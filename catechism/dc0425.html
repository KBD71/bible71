Westminster Confession of Faith 9.4

When God converts a sinner, and translates him into the state of grace, He frees him from his natural bondage under sin; and, by His grace alone, enables him freely to will and to do that which is spiritually good; yet so, as that by reason of his remaining corruption, he does not perfectly, nor only, will that which is good, but does also will that which is evil.

Key Scriptures

"For he has rescued us from the dominion of darkness and brought us into the kingdom of the Son he loves." (Colossians 1:13)

"For it is God who works in you to will and to act in order to fulfill his good purpose." (Philippians 2:13)

"For the flesh desires what is contrary to the Spirit, and the Spirit what is contrary to the flesh... so that you are not to do whatever you want." (Galatians 5:17)

"For I do not do the good I want to do, but the evil I do not want to do—this I keep on doing." (Romans 7:19)

01. From Bondage to Spiritual Liberty

The natural man is enslaved to a corrupt nature, incapable of choosing what is spiritually pleasing to God. The transition to the state of grace involves two inseparable acts: the conversion of the sinner and their translation into grace.

  • A Liberation: God breaks the shackles of our fallen nature, enabling us to truly love Him and seek His will for the first time.
  • B Sovereign Act: This change is initiated by God alone ("By Grace Alone"). It is not earned by merit but is a direct result of the Holy Spirit's work within the believer.

02. The Paradox of Remaining Corruption

Though the believer is free, they are not yet perfect. The Confession honestly addresses the "sad reality" of the Christian life: the coexistence of spiritual good and lingering sin.

The regenerate person truly desires to follow God's law. However, because of indwelling sin, they also find themselves desiring what is evil. This internal struggle is not a sign of a lack of salvation, but rather the evidence of a new life fighting against the old.

"The law of my mind" versus "the law of sin" (Romans 7:23).

03. The Mark of the Regenerate Will

Why does the Bible say a believer "wants" the good even when they fail? It is because willing the spiritual good is the unique hallmark of the state of grace.

  • Innocence vs. Grace: In the state of innocence, man could "not sin." In the state of sin, man "cannot but sin." In the state of grace, man is "enabled to choose the good" despite the presence of sin.
  • Spiritual Vitality: The ability to sincerely repent and seek holiness is a supernatural gift that no unregenerate person possesses.
  • Hope: We are encouraged to persevere, knowing that the Spirit who began this work will eventually bring our will to a state of perfect, immutable holiness in glory.
