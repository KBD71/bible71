Westminster Confession of Faith 9.1

God has endued the will of man with that natural liberty, that it is neither forced, nor by any absolute necessity of nature determined to good or evil.

Key Scriptures

"I tell you, Elijah has already come, and they did not recognize him, but have done to him whatever they wished." (Matthew 17:12)

"But each person is tempted when they are dragged away by their own evil desire and enticed." (James 1:14)

"...I have set before you life and death, blessings and curses. Now choose life, so that you and your children may live." (Deuteronomy 30:19)

01. The Definition of Natural Liberty

The "will" mentioned in the Confession refers to the capacity of a person—possessing reason, emotion, and volition—to make choices, judgments, or decisions.

  • A Inherent Gift: This natural liberty is a faculty granted by God to the human soul. Every human being possesses this freedom of will by nature.
  • B Persistence After the Fall: Even in a fallen state, the essential nature of the will remains free. We do not lose the faculty of willing itself, though our ability to choose the spiritual good is corrupted.

02. Free Will is Not Forced

"Not being forced" implies two critical dimensions of human action:

No External Compulsion

Even when external circumstances exert strong pressure, the will itself remains the agent of choice. We are not robots moved solely by outward forces.

Internal Harmony with Reason

The intellect presents what is "good" or "true," but the will is not "forced" by the intellect. It chooses to desire what the mind perceives, acting with voluntary spontaneity.

03. Rejection of Absolute Necessity

The Confession denies Determinism—the idea that our choices are pre-programmed by an inescapable necessity that bypasses the will.

  • The Divine Example: God always does good because He is good. His "necessity" of being holy is in perfect harmony with His "liberty" of willing. He chooses the good most freely.
  • Moral Responsibility: Because the will acts according to its own nature without being determined by a blind, external fate, humans are justly held responsible for their actions, whether good or evil.
  • Spontaneous Action: We choose what we want because we want it, not because we are forced by an irresistible mechanical law.
